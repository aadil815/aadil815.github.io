From hidden contamination to uneven water bills to measurable reform.

This project connects three linked stories on Long Island and in New York State: the discovery of widespread 1,4-dioxane contamination in consumer products, the unequal and often opaque cost of residential water across Long Island, and the measurable results of state intervention a few years later.

1. The problem: contamination was hidden, widespread, and flowing back into the water supply.

In 2018, Citizens Campaign for the Environment found 1,4-dioxane in more than 80% of tested personal care products. The contaminant was especially concerning because it was not intentionally added, was not listed on labels, and could enter Long Island's aquifer system through wastewater and septic systems after everyday household use.

That made this more than a consumer safety issue. It was also a drinking water issue. New York later responded with a first-of-its-kind law that limited 1,4-dioxane in cleaning, cosmetic, and personal care products, alongside an aggressive drinking water standard.

2. The body of the story: what water cost on Long Island in 2019, and why that mattered.

The 2019 report asked a deceptively simple question: what does water cost on Long Island? The answer turned out to be highly uneven. CCE compared 48 districts and found that consumers faced a confusing mix of rate systems, taxes, service charges, privatized service areas, and billing structures that made apples-to-apples comparisons unusually hard. The result was not just variation, but opacity.

🔎 What the report revealed
  • The most expensive district in the report was NY American Water Area 2 in North Shore–Sea Cliff at $1,124.52 annually, while Greenlawn was the least expensive at $148.00.
  • Several districts had large tax burdens layered on top of rates, including Albertson, Bethpage, Garden City Park, Carle Place, Roslyn, and Hicksville.
  • The report flagged multiple waterless districts and fifteen districts serving fewer than 10,000 people, strengthening the argument for consolidation.
  • CCE also criticized confusing pricing systems such as daily rate structures, cubic-feet billing, and hard-to-find fee schedules.
  • Privatization was directly tied to cost concerns, especially in New York American Water service areas that appeared repeatedly among the most expensive districts.

3. The result: New York's law changed product chemistry, and the follow-up data shows it.

By 2024, CCE's follow-up testing showed clear evidence that the law worked. New York had already set a 1 ppb drinking water standard, then required products sold in the state to reduce 1,4-dioxane to 2,000 ppb in 2023 and 1,000 ppb in 2024. The five detergents re-tested by CCE all fell below the 1,000 ppb threshold.

That matters because these products were not marginal cases. They had been among the highest-contaminant products in the original dataset and were part of the evidence used to justify regulation.
