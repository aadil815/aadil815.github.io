The Temporal Dynamics of the Neural Response to Social Rewards

An honors thesis project examining whether the neural value of shared rewards persists over time in social versus non social contexts. Using a secondary analysis of task-based fMRI data, this study tested habituation patterns in the ventral striatum and explored whole-brain temporal effects linked to shared social reward.

Primary domain: Social neuroscience
Methods: fMRI, GLM, ROI, whole-brain contrasts
Software: fMRIPrep, FSL FEAT

Project Overview

Positive social interactions are rewarding, but a stronger reward signal does not necessarily mean a more persistent one. This thesis asked whether rewards shared with other people, especially close friends, would remain neurally elevated across time relative to rewards shared with a computer. The original thesis was revised here into a tighter portfolio format while preserving the same findings, evidence base, and source framework.

Research Question

Do shared social rewards produce more sustained neural activation over time than non social rewards, and does this persistence differ when rewards are shared with a close friend versus other partners?

Hypothesis

Ventral striatal responses to social reward, particularly rewards shared with friends, were expected to habituate more slowly than responses observed in non social conditions.

Study Design

Analytic Pipeline

  • Task based fMRI preprocessing completed with fMRIPrep.
  • Group level statistical modeling performed in FSL FEAT.
  • Nine regressors of interest modeled outcome conditions across partner types.
  • Linear trend weights across runs were used to test sustained activity over time.

Why this matters

Prior work shows that close social partners can amplify reward value. This project examined a more specific question: whether that enhanced value signal remains stable across repeated exposures or diminishes with time.

Results

The central ventral striatum hypothesis was not supported. However, whole-brain analyses revealed sustained activation in cortical regions associated with cognitive control and social evaluation.

Key Findings

  • The predicted sustained ventral striatal effect for social reward was not observed.
  • Whole-brain results identified sustained social reward effects in lateral prefrontal and parietal regions.
  • Friend versus computer contrasts localized a more specific sustained cortical response to close social reward.

Interpretation

The findings suggest that the ventral striatum may track the immediate subjective value of reward without necessarily carrying its persistence across repeated exposures. In this study, temporal persistence appeared more strongly in distributed cortical regions implicated in cognitive control, attention, and socially relevant evaluation.

This makes the null ventral striatum result informative rather than merely negative: the data point to a functional distinction between initial reward valuation and sustained social reward processing.

Methods Snapshot

Experimental details

  • Two-day protocol with closeness measures collected before scanning.
  • Close friend photographs and partner identities were embedded into the task.
  • Predetermined outcomes ensured equal reward and punishment exposure.
  • Participants alternated between player and observer roles across runs.

Imaging and modeling

  • Structural and functional images were acquired on a 3T Siemens Allegra scanner.
  • Analyses used a general linear model with outcome phase regressors across partner conditions.
  • Missed trials were modeled as regressors of no interest.
  • Linear trend weights were applied to test sustained activation across runs.

Limitations and Rigor

Limitations

  • Substantial motion-related exclusion reduced the final sample size.
  • The final analyzed sample included only female MRI participants.
  • Fixed effects group analysis limits population-level generalization.
  • The project used a secondary dataset rather than a study designed from the outset around temporal persistence.

Why the project still has value

Even without support for the original ventral striatum prediction, the thesis contributes a careful and honest interpretation of a null ROI result alongside meaningful whole-brain findings. That combination reflects strong research judgment: the project did not force the hypothesis, and it extracted a more precise neuroscientific insight from the observed data.
