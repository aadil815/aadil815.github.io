The Evolution of Genes Composing the Lamina Propria and Their Effect on Vocalization in Tigers

This project examines whether four genes tied to the lamina propria of tiger vocal folds show evidence of selection linked to roaring ability. Across the report and slide deck, two genes stand out as the main evolutionary signals: COL1A1 and ELN.

Why this question matters

Tigers produce exceptionally low frequency, high intensity roars that place large mechanical stresses on the vocal folds. The study asks whether genes involved in connective tissue structure and elasticity helped shape this specialized roaring system.

The biological framing is elegant: if roaring requires tissue that can tolerate extreme strain, then genes shaping collagen, elastin, and the extracellular matrix become plausible evolutionary targets.

Study focus

The analysis centered on genes associated with the lamina propria of the vocal folds, combining rooted phylogenetic trees with branch model comparisons between null and alternative selection models.

Interpretation

The web version organizes the findings around a simple narrative: collagen structure plus tissue elasticity likely matter most for roaring adaptation. COL1A1 speaks to tensile architecture, while ELN points to elastic recoil. Together, they form the most convincing story in the study.

Structural mechanics

Roaring places large stress on the vocal folds, so tissue architecture is a reasonable evolutionary target.

Collagen signal

COL1A1 produced the largest likelihood ratio, making it the strongest signal in the project.

Elasticity signal

ELN also showed a strong departure from the null, fitting the expectation that elastic fibers matter in repeated oscillation.

Negative cases matter too

COL1A2 and NID1 help sharpen the interpretation because not every lamina propria gene showed the same pattern.
