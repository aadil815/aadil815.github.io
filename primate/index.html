The Negative Selection of Leptin in Various Primate Species in Relation to Menopause

This study investigates the evolutionary history of leptin, a protein hormone involved in metabolic and reproductive function. Using phylogenetic analysis, the McDonald-Kreitman test, and dN/dS branch model analysis, the report concludes that leptin underwent substantial negative selection in humans.

Introduction

Leptin regulates energy homeostasis, reproductive function, and metabolic balance. Because menopause is tied to changing reproductive physiology, this study examined whether the evolutionary history of leptin in humans differed from that of other primates.

Study focus

The report tested whether human leptin had undergone positive selection relative to other primate species, including gorillas and orangutans. Instead, the final evidence supported the opposite interpretation.

Species set
13
Human plus 12 other primate species in the analysis
Gene target

Methods

Three complementary analyses were used to evaluate the evolutionary pattern of leptin across primates.

Phylogenetic analysis

Compared leptin sequences across primates to assess relatedness and branch divergence.

McDonald-Kreitman test

Compared polymorphism within humans to divergence between humans and chimpanzees.

dN/dS branch model

Tested whether human leptin evolved differently from the other primate branches.

Comparative interpretation

Integrated statistical and phylogenetic evidence rather than relying on a single metric.

Phylogenetic result

Human leptin was found to be most closely related to leptin in gorillas and chimpanzees. The tree also showed divergence involving orangutans and white-cheeked gibbons relative to the rest of the primates.

McDonald-Kreitman test

The report recorded 3 nonsynonymous polymorphisms and 1 synonymous polymorphism, along with 3 synonymous and 1 nonsynonymous human-chimpanzee differences. Because Dn/Ds < Pn/Ps, the result was interpreted as evidence of negative selection.

Pn / Ps3 / 1 = 3.0
Dn / Ds1 / 3 ≈ 0.33

dN/dS branch model

The likelihood ratio was below the significance threshold, so the null hypothesis was accepted. This indicated that dN/dS was not significantly different in the human lineage relative to the other primates.

Conclusion

The hypothesis of positive selection was rejected. Across the phylogenetic analysis, McDonald-Kreitman test, and dN/dS branch model, the evidence supported the conclusion that human leptin has undergone negative selection. This suggests leptin has maintained a consistent and important physiological role in human metabolic and reproductive functioning before the onset of menopause.
